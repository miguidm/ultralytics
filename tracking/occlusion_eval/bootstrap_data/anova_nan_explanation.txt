================================================================================
 UNDERSTANDING NaN IN ANOVA RESULTS AND STATISTICAL DESIGN CHOICES
================================================================================

When analyzing the ANOVA output tables, "NaN" (Not a Number) frequently appears. 
It appears for two distinct mathematical reasons, one of which is standard, and the 
other which dictated the experimental design of this ablation study.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 1. NaN in the "Residual" Row (Standard Output)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Example Output:
                           sum_sq    df          F        PR(>F)
C(Version)               0.020316   1.0  43.841391  1.819649e-07
C(Placement)             0.015014   3.0  10.800095  4.651949e-05
C(Version):C(Placement)  0.022821   3.0  16.415880  1.228240e-06
Residual                 0.014828  32.0        NaN           NaN

What it means:
The "Residual" represents the random noise or error remaining after the ANOVA 
accounts for your variables (Version and Placement). The F-statistic (F) and the 
p-value (PR(>F)) for your variables are calculated by dividing their variance by 
the variance of the Residual. Because a residual cannot be divided by itself to test 
itself, statistical software outputs NaN for those columns.

How to report this in the paper:
This is standard mathematical behavior. When formatting the ANOVA tables for the 
final paper, the NaN values in the Residual row should be left blank or replaced 
with dashes (-). No explanation of this is required in the methodology or results.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 2. NaN in Main Variables (Matrix Rank Deficiency)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Example Output (When attempting to include Vanilla in the Two-Way ANOVA):
                           sum_sq    df          F        PR(>F)
C(Version)                    NaN   2.0        NaN           NaN
C(Placement)             0.010679   4.0   6.151921  1.793878e-02

What it means:
This occurs when the ANOVA attempts to calculate an interaction effect on a dataset 
that is not "fully crossed". A Two-Way ANOVA requires every category in Variable 1 
to intersect with every category in Variable 2. 

While the deformable variants possess structural placements (Neck-FPN, Neck-PAN, 
Backbone), the Vanilla baseline inherently lacks these deformable placement categories. 
Because a "Vanilla model with a deformable FPN neck" does not exist, the mathematical 
matrix contains empty cells. When the ANOVA algorithm attempts to calculate the 
interaction across those empty cells, the calculation fails, resulting in a 
"Matrix Rank Deficiency" and outputting NaN for the entire variable.

How to explain this to a thesis panel or peer reviewers:
If asked why the Vanilla baseline was evaluated separately from the main Two-Way ANOVA, 
the following explanation should be used:

"The Vanilla baseline was excluded from the main Two-Way ANOVA to prevent matrix rank 
deficiency. A standard Two-Way ANOVA requires a fully-crossed factorial design. Because 
the standard Vanilla architecture inherently lacks the structural placement categories 
(e.g., Neck-FPN, Neck-PAN) being evaluated in the deformable variants, its inclusion 
results in empty interaction cells that crash the mathematical model (outputting NaN). 
Therefore, the ablation study was separated into two statistically sound phases: first, 
a Two-Way ANOVA strictly isolating the architectural mechanics of the deformable models; 
and second, a targeted One-Way ANOVA and Tukey's HSD post-hoc test to compare those 
optimized configurations against the Vanilla baseline."
